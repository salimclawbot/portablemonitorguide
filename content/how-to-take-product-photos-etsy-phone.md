## Understanding Light: Your Most Important Tool

Light is everything in product photography. Professional results don't come from expensive cameras — they come from understanding and controlling light.

### Natural Light vs. Artificial Light

Natural light is the gold standard for Etsy product photography because it's free, beautiful, and flatters almost every product. A south-facing window (north-facing for Northern Hemisphere readers) provides consistent, indirect light that creates soft shadows and highlights without harsh glare.

Why south-facing? In Australia, the sun moves across the northern sky, so south-facing windows receive indirect light — no direct sun glare. For Northern Hemisphere readers, north-facing windows serve this purpose.

### The Best Time of Day to Shoot

**Morning and late afternoon** produce the most flattering light. Avoid midday when the sun is directly overhead and creates harsh shadows. If you only have access to a north-facing window (in Australia) or south-facing window (in the Northern Hemisphere), morning and afternoon are even more critical.

## Setting Up Your DIY Studio

Professional product photography studios can cost thousands. Your DIY version costs under $50.

### What You Need

- Your smartphone (any recent model works)
- A south-facing window (north-facing for Northern Hemisphere readers)
- White poster board or foam core ($5)
- A small table or box ($0 if you use what you have)
- A tripod or DIY phone holder ($10-15)
- Optional: reflector or white cloth to bounce light ($5)

### The Basic Setup

1. Position your table perpendicular to your south-facing window (north-facing for Northern Hemisphere readers), about 2-3 feet away
2. Create a "backdrop" by leaning white poster board against something behind your product
3. Use another piece of white board opposite the window to bounce light back into shadows
4. Position your phone on a tripod, about 12-18 inches from your product

This setup takes 10 minutes to create and produces results that look professional.

## Smartphone Camera Settings That Work

Modern smartphones have powerful cameras that most users never fully utilize.

### Enable These Settings

- **Use Portrait Mode** for a shallow depth of field that blurs the background and makes your product pop
- **Enable Grid** (in camera settings) to help with composition using the rule of thirds
- **Turn off digital zoom** — move closer instead. Digital zoom degrades quality dramatically
- **Use Natural Light mode** if your phone offers it, which adjusts white balance automatically

### The Exposure Triangle Simplified

On your smartphone:
- **Brightness** (tap the screen to focus, then slide up/down to adjust)
- **Autofocus** (tap on your product to ensure it's in focus)
- **No need to touch ISO or shutter speed** — your phone handles these automatically

## Composition Techniques That Sell

Great product photos follow specific composition rules that draw the eye and build desire.

### Rule of Thirds

Don't center your product dead-center in the frame. Instead:
1. Imagine a grid dividing your image into 9 equal squares (enable this in camera settings)
2. Place your product on one of the four intersection points
3. This creates visual balance and is more engaging than centered composition

### Angle and Perspective

- **Straight-on:** Best for flat products (artwork, textiles)
- **3/4 angle:** Best for most items (jewelry, ceramics, home goods) — shows product shape and dimension
- **Overhead:** Excellent for food, collections, or showing product in context
- **Close-up detail:** Always include at least one shot showing texture or craftsmanship details

### Negative Space

Don't feel pressured to fill the entire frame. Often, a simple background with generous negative space (empty space around the product) makes the product more prominent and professional.

## Lighting Scenarios: How to Handle Every Situation

### Scenario 1: Bright Afternoon Light

**The Problem:** Direct sun creates harsh shadows
**The Solution:** 
- Move further from the window or use sheer curtains to diffuse light
- Use a white reflector opposite the light source to fill shadows
- Shoot between 3-5 PM when light is lower and softer

### Scenario 2: Overcast Day

**The Problem:** Flat, even light lacks dimension
**The Solution:**
- Overcast light is actually ideal for product photography — it's naturally diffused
- Use white reflectors to add subtle dimension
- Ensure your background creates contrast with your product

### Scenario 3: Limited Window Access

**The Problem:** You only have a small or north-facing window
**The Solution:**
- Use multiple windows if possible
- Invest in a basic LED light panel ($20-30)
- Shoot during morning or late afternoon for the best angles

## Styling Your Product for Maximum Appeal

The product itself is part of your composition.

### Create Context

- Show your item in use: jewelry worn, a mug with flowers, a blanket draped
- Include complementary items: styling props that frame your product
- Tell a story: lifestyle shots sell better than blank-background shots

Sellers report that listings with 7–10 photos convert significantly better than listings with only 1-3 photos. Variety matters — include lifestyle shots, detail shots, and clean product-only shots.

### Props and Styling

- Keep props minimal and on-brand
- Use neutral colors that don't compete with your product
- Wooden surfaces, plants, and neutral fabrics work well for most items
- Avoid anything that distract from the product itself

## Editing Your Photos: The Right Way

Editing isn't cheating — it's correction and enhancement.

### What to Edit

1. **Brightness and Contrast** — ensure your product is properly exposed
2. **Saturation** — boost color slightly (but don't oversaturate)
3. **Sharpness** — make details crisp
4. **White Balance** — correct any color casts from artificial light

### What NOT to Edit

- Don't dramatically change the color of your product
- Don't remove imperfections that are honest representations
- Don't add effects or filters that make photos look fake

### Free Editing Apps That Work

- **Snapseed** (Google) — excellent for brightness, saturation, detail
- **VSCO** — preset filters that enhance without overdoing it
- **Adobe Lightroom Mobile** — professional-grade editing, free version sufficient

### The Rule: Edit for Clarity, Not for Fantasy

Your photos should represent your product accurately. A customer who receives an item that looks different from the photo will leave a negative review. Honest, well-lit, properly edited photos build trust and reduce returns.

## Common Mistakes Etsy Sellers Make

### Mistake 1: Shooting in Poor Light and Over-Editing

This creates images that look fake and don't match the actual product in person. Invest in lighting instead.

### Mistake 2: Using Only Close-Up Shots

Customers want to see the full item AND detail shots. Include both.

### Mistake 3: Cluttered Backgrounds

A busy background competes for attention with your product. Simplify.

### Mistake 4: Using Digital Zoom

Always move closer physically rather than zooming digitally. Digital zoom degrades sharpness.

### Mistake 5: Inconsistent Styling Across Photos

If photo 1 has a white background and photo 2 has wood background and photo 3 has fabric, the listing looks disorganized. Choose a consistent style.

## The 10-Photo Shot List for Etsy Success

Here's exactly what to photograph:

1. **Full product, clean background** — main listing image
2. **3/4 angle** — shows dimension
3. **Close-up of detail or texture** — shows craftsmanship
4. **Lifestyle/in-use shot** — shows it in context
5. **Top-down view** — alternative perspective
6. **With scale reference** (coin, hand) — shows actual size
7. **Back or alternative side** — shows all angles
8. **Texture/fabric close-up** — if relevant to product
9. **Packaging or bundled view** — if selling multiples
10. **Alternative styling** — shows versatility

## FAQ

**Can I use my phone camera or do I need a professional camera?**
Your phone camera is professional enough. The iPhone 12 and later, and most current Android flagships, take product photos indistinguishable from DSLR cameras when properly lit. The difference is light, not equipment.

**How many photos should I include?**
Sellers report that listings with 7–10 photos convert significantly better than listings with fewer photos. Include variety: full product, details, lifestyle, and alternative angles.

**What if my product is shiny and reflects too much light?**
Use diffusers. White tissue paper, parchment paper, or frosted plastic placed between your light source and product softens reflections dramatically.

**Should I edit in black and white?**
Only if your product is genuinely suited to black and white. Color usually sells better because it shows your product's actual appearance.

**Do I need to buy expensive editing software?**
No. Free apps like Snapseed and VSCO are sufficient for product photography editing.
