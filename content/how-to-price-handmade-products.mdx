# How to Price Handmade Products for Profit

**By Cara Novak, Pricing Strategist & Etsy Coach | Last updated March 2026**

**Pricing handmade products correctly is the single most important skill a craft business owner can develop. Underpricing is the number one reason talented makers go out of business. The right price covers all your costs, pays you fairly for your time, builds in business profit, and positions your work at its true value in the market. This guide gives you the exact formulas, real examples, and mindset shifts you need to price with confidence.**

## Why Most Handmade Sellers Undercharge {#undercharging}

<figure>
  <img src="/images/crafty/underpricing-handmade-products.jpg" alt="Handmade ceramic mug next to a calculator showing how underpricing fails to cover true costs including labour time" loading="lazy" />
  <figcaption>The true cost of handmade work: most sellers forget to include their own time, platform fees, packaging, and overhead when setting prices</figcaption>
</figure>

Before we get to formulas, let's diagnose the problem. After working with over 600 handmade sellers, I've identified five patterns that drive chronic underpricing:

### 1. "I Don't Want to Charge Too Much"

This stems from impostor syndrome — the belief that your work isn't "good enough" to command a real price. Meanwhile, your Etsy competitors who charge twice what you do aren't necessarily better; they're just more confident in their pricing.

### 2. Forgetting to Count Their Own Time

This is the most common error. Makers calculate materials cost, then add a small margin, completely ignoring that they spent 3 hours making the item. If your labour isn't in the price, you're literally paying to run your business.

### 3. Pricing Based on What Feels "Fair" to the Customer

Empathising with your customer's wallet is admirable but financially fatal. You do not know what your customer's financial situation is. Many people who buy handmade *specifically* want to pay more to support a maker.

### 4. Competing with Mass-Produced Alternatives

Handmade candles cannot compete on price with Kmart candles. That's not the market. Handmade buyers are paying for craft, uniqueness, quality, and story. Price accordingly.

### 5. Not Accounting for Platform Fees

Etsy takes 6.5% transaction fee + listing fees + payment processing (~3–4%). That's potentially 10–11% of your retail price gone before you even look at costs. Many sellers forget this entirely.

## The Core Pricing Formula {#core-formula}

<figure>
  <img src="/images/crafty/pricing-formula-breakdown.jpg" alt="Whiteboard diagram showing the handmade pricing formula: materials plus labour plus overhead times 2 equals wholesale, wholesale times 2 equals retail" loading="lazy" />
  <figcaption>The keystone markup formula used by professional handmade businesses: each stage doubles to ensure profitability at every sales level</figcaption>
</figure>

Here is the foundation every handmade business should use:

<div style="background:#f0fdf4;border:2px solid #16a34a;border-radius:12px;padding:2rem;margin:2rem 0;text-align:center;">
  <h3 style="margin:0 0 1rem;color:#15803d;font-size:1.3rem;">The Handmade Pricing Formula</h3>
  <p style="font-size:1.1rem;margin:0.5rem 0;"><strong>Cost of Goods (COG) = Materials + Labour + Overhead</strong></p>
  <p style="font-size:1.1rem;margin:0.5rem 0;"><strong>Wholesale Price = COG × 2</strong></p>
  <p style="font-size:1.1rem;margin:0.5rem 0;"><strong>Retail Price = Wholesale × 2</strong></p>
  <p style="color:#15803d;font-weight:700;font-size:1.2rem;margin:1rem 0 0;">→ Retail Price = COG × 4</p>
</div>

This is called the **keystone markup model**, and it's the industry standard for product-based businesses.

### Why 4x?

The 4x multiplier on your cost of goods isn't arbitrary. Here's what it covers:

- **2x for wholesale:** Allows you to sell wholesale to retailers while still being profitable
- **2x for retail:** Covers your direct selling costs (platform fees, marketing, packaging) and builds actual profit

If you only sell direct (Etsy, markets, your own website), a minimum 3x markup is workable — but 4x gives you room for discounts, sales events, and business investment.

### Worked Example: Ceramic Mug

| Cost Component | Calculation | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Clay | 200g per mug × $0.08/g | $16.00 |
| Glazes | Estimated per unit | $3.50 |
| Firing cost | Gas/electricity per unit | $4.00 |
| Packaging | Box + tissue + label | $2.50 |
| **Materials Total** | | **$26.00** |
| Labour | 2.5 hours × $35/hr | $87.50 |
| Overhead (20% of materials) | $26.00 × 0.20 | $5.20 |
| **Cost of Goods** | | **$118.70** |
| **Wholesale Price** | COG × 2 | **$237.40** |
| **Retail Price** | Wholesale × 2 | **$474.80 → ~$479** |

Many makers would look at that number and panic. "Nobody will pay $479 for a mug!" But here's the thing: handmade ceramics in this price range sell every day on Etsy and at markets. If your work looks like $479 ceramics (quality photos, compelling story, strong branding), buyers will pay it. If it looks like a $35 market-stall mug, it won't sell regardless of quality.

## Calculating Your True Costs {#calculating-costs}

<figure>
  <img src="/images/crafty/cost-tracking-spreadsheet.jpg" alt="Spreadsheet showing handmade product cost tracking including materials, packaging, and platform fees for accurate pricing" loading="lazy" />
  <figcaption>A proper cost tracking spreadsheet: every material component must be costed at the per-unit level, including consumables and packaging</figcaption>
</figure>

### Materials: Going Beyond the Obvious

Most makers only include the primary raw material. Here's what you must track:

**Direct Materials:**
- Primary raw material (clay, yarn, fabric, wax, etc.)
- Secondary materials (dyes, pigments, findings, clasps)
- Packaging (boxes, bags, tissue, ribbon, labels)
- Shipping materials (if included in your product price)

**How to calculate accurately:**
Don't guess. Weigh your clay. Count your beads. Track what you use per unit over a full batch and divide. Many materials cost calculators exist — see our roundup of [best Etsy SEO tools for 2026](/best-etsy-seo-tools-2026) which includes some that have built-in cost tracking.

**Consumables people forget:**
- Sandpaper, polishing pads (slowly consumed)
- Tool replacement costs (blades, tips)
- Cleaning materials
- Printer ink, tags, stickers

### The Per-Unit Method

Track costs at the batch level, then divide:

- Make a batch of 20 candles
- Total batch materials: $140
- Per-unit materials cost: $7.00
- Don't forget to add packaging per unit separately

## Setting Your Labour Rate {#labour-rate}

<figure>
  <img src="/images/crafty/labour-rate-calculator.jpg" alt="Calculator and notebook showing hourly labour rate calculation for handmade business owner including desired annual salary and business hours" loading="lazy" />
  <figcaption>Calculate your true hourly rate based on the annual income you need, not just minimum wage — you're running a skilled business</figcaption>
</figure>

Your labour rate is the amount per hour you pay yourself for making time. This is separate from the profit your business makes.

### How to Set Your Rate

**Minimum floor:** The Australian minimum wage is approximately $24.10/hour (2025–26). This is your absolute floor, not your target.

**For skilled artisan work:** $35–$60/hour is appropriate for work requiring significant skill development or years of practice.

**For highly specialised work:** $60–$100/hour is legitimate for complex techniques with long mastery curves.

**The annual salary method:**

If you want to take home $60,000 per year from your business:
- 52 weeks × 25 making-hours/week = 1,300 billable hours
- $60,000 ÷ 1,300 hours = **$46.15/hour labour rate**

### Time Tracking is Non-Negotiable

You cannot accurately price your labour if you're guessing how long things take. Use a simple timer (your phone) for 2–4 weeks to track actual making time for each product. Most makers discover they're working faster or slower than they estimated.

**Include all time:** design iterations, cutting, assembly, finishing, drying/curing time (if you're waiting on it), quality checking, and packaging.

## Understanding Overhead {#overhead}

Overhead is the cost of running your business that doesn't attach directly to a single product.

### What Counts as Overhead

- **Platform fees:** Etsy listing fees ($0.27 each), transaction fees (6.5%), payment processing (~3–4%)
- **Subscription costs:** Etsy Plus, Canva, accounting software
- **Studio/workspace costs:** Rent, utilities for your making space
- **Equipment depreciation:** Your tools wear out — a $500 kiln amortised over 5 years = $100/year
- **Marketing:** Photography equipment, prop costs, advertising
- **Business insurance**
- **Accountant/bookkeeper fees**
- **Business banking fees**

### How to Include Overhead in Your Price

**Method 1 — Percentage markup:** Calculate total monthly overhead, divide by number of units you sell per month, add to each unit.

**Method 2 — Overhead rate:** Common shorthand is to add 10–20% of your materials cost as overhead to each product.

**Example:**
Monthly overhead costs: $450
Average units sold per month: 45
Overhead per unit: $10

Or: If materials = $26, overhead at 15% = $3.90/unit

Use whichever method is simpler for your business, but **use one consistently**.

### Price Consistency Across Channels

**Important rule:** Keep your retail price consistent across channels, especially if you sell on both Etsy and your own website. Customers who find you at a market will check online. Inconsistent pricing creates confusion and distrust.

## Psychological Pricing Strategies {#psychology}

<figure>
  <img src="/images/crafty/psychological-pricing-handmade.jpg" alt="Three price tags showing charm pricing ending in 9 versus round numbers and premium flat pricing strategies for handmade goods" loading="lazy" />
  <figcaption>Price psychology: charm pricing ($49 vs $50) works for impulse buys; round numbers signal premium quality for high-end artisan pieces</figcaption>
</figure>

### Charm Pricing ($X9 endings)

Prices ending in 9 ($29, $79, $149) are perceived as lower than the next round number. This works well for lower-to-mid price points and impulse purchases under $100.

### Round Number Pricing for Premium Items

At higher price points ($200+), round numbers ($250, $380, $500) actually signal quality and confidence. "Here is what my work costs. I'm not negotiating by the dollar."

### Price Anchoring

Show the premium version first. If your mugs come in two sizes — $65 and $95 — list the $95 version first. The $65 then looks like the accessible option.

### Bundle Pricing

A 3-piece candle set for $85 vs $32 each. The bundle provides apparent value, you move more inventory at once, and the effective per-unit margin is maintained.

## When to Raise Your Prices {#raising-prices}

<figure>
  <img src="/images/crafty/when-to-raise-prices-handmade.jpg" alt="Etsy shop analytics dashboard showing conversion rate and sell-through metrics that indicate when prices are too low" loading="lazy" />
  <figcaption>When your items sell too quickly or your conversion rate is unusually high, it often signals your prices are below market rate</figcaption>
</figure>

You should raise your prices when:

**You're selling out quickly.** If items sell in days, you've priced below market. Raise 10–15%.

**Customers aren't haggling.** If no one asks for discounts, you may have room to go higher.

**Your conversion rate is very high.** Above 5–8% on Etsy suggests pricing may be leaving money on the table.

**Your costs have increased.** Material price increases must be passed on. Don't absorb inflation.

**You've improved your craft.** Your skill level two years in should command more than when you started.

**You're not making a profit.** This is the most important signal. Use the formula. If the numbers don't work at your current price, raise the price or cut the product.

### How to Raise Prices Without Losing Customers

- **Raise gradually** — 10–15% increases are less noticeable than sudden large jumps
- **Add value first** — improved packaging, additional content, better photography
- **Tell your story** — "As my craft has grown, I've invested in better materials and my prices reflect that"
- **Run a "price goes up on [date]" sale** — many customers will buy before the increase

## FAQ {#faq}

### What is the standard formula for pricing handmade products?

The most widely used formula is: (Materials + Labour + Overhead) × 2 = Wholesale Price. Wholesale × 2 = Retail Price. This keystone markup ensures you cover all costs and build in profit at both direct and wholesale selling levels.

### How much should I pay myself per hour for handmade work?

Pay yourself at minimum the living wage for your area — in Australia, approximately $25–$30/hour. For skilled artisan work, $40–$60/hour is appropriate. Never price labour below minimum wage.

### Why are my handmade products not selling even at a low price?

Price alone rarely determines sales. More common culprits are poor product photography, weak SEO (product titles and tags), insufficient listing descriptions, or selling in a saturated niche without differentiation.

### Should I charge less on Etsy to compete with cheap alternatives?

No. Competing on price with mass-produced alternatives is a losing strategy for handmade sellers. Your competitive advantage is uniqueness, quality, and story — not price.

### What profit margin should handmade products have?

Target a minimum 30% profit margin after all costs including your labour. A 40–50% margin gives you room for sales, discounts, and business reinvestment. Many successful handmade sellers operate at 50–70% margins.
